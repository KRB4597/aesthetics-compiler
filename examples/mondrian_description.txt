Mondrian Composition II in Red, Blue, and Yellow (1930)

An oil painting by Piet Mondrian, one of the defining works of De Stijl and Neoplasticism. The composition is rigorously geometric, organized around a grid of thick black horizontal and vertical lines that divide the white canvas into rectangular fields of varying proportions.

Three of the rectangles are filled with primary colors: a large red square occupies the upper right quadrant, a small blue rectangle sits in the lower left corner, and a small yellow rectangle anchors the lower right. The remaining fields are left white, providing negative space and visual breathing room.

The composition is asymmetric but perfectly balanced. The heavy red field on the right is counterweighted by the spatial mass of the large white fields on the left. The result is a dynamic equilibrium that feels both restless and resolved — a hallmark of Mondrian's mature style.

The palette is deliberately restricted to the three primary colors plus black and white. There is virtually no texture, no gestural mark, and no representation. The composition achieves maximum order through the grid structure while maintaining visual interest through the asymmetric placement of color fields.

This painting exemplifies De Stijl principles: reduction to primary elements, rejection of organic form, and the search for universal harmony through pure geometric relationships.
