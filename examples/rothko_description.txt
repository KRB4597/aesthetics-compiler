Rothko No. 61 (Rust and Blue)

A large-format oil on canvas by Mark Rothko in his signature color field style. The composition presents two luminous, soft-edged rectangular fields of color stacked vertically against a dark background.

The upper field is a deep rust-orange, saturated and warm, occupying roughly half the canvas. Its edges are deliberately blurred and feathered, dissolving into the surrounding space rather than asserting a hard boundary. The surface is built up with many thin translucent glazes, creating a sense of radiant inner light.

The lower field is a dark, slightly greenish blue — cooler, denser, and heavier than the form above. It anchors the composition. The two rectangles maintain a tense equilibrium: the warm upper field and the cool lower field do not harmonize easily, yet the carefully tuned saturation levels create a meditative balance.

The overall palette is limited and nearly monochromatic within each field. The composition is extremely simple — two forms, almost no complexity — but the surface handling adds subtle texture. The scale is immersive; at close range the canvas exceeds the viewer's peripheral vision, producing a sensation of being surrounded by color.

The painting exemplifies abstract expressionism through its rejection of representational imagery, emphasis on pure color relationships, and the viewer's emotional and physiological response to scale and hue.
